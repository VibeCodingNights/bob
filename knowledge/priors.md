# Hackathon Priors
Accumulated patterns for strategic analysis. Injected into strategy synthesis prompts.

## Platform patterns
- **Devpost (online/async judging):** Judges evaluate asynchronously from submissions, so the README, demo video, and written description carry disproportionate weight vs. live charisma. A polished 2-3 minute video with clear problem-solution framing is often the primary evaluation artifact. Submissions can be updated until the deadline — submit early, then iterate on documentation. Some events offer early-submission bonus prizes.
- **Devpost (corporate-sponsored):** Corporate Devpost hackathons reward deep API integration with sponsor products. Sponsor DevRel teams often judge their own prize tracks and look for creative use cases they can feature in blog posts and quarterly reports. The submission README functions as marketing collateral for the sponsor.
- **ETHGlobal:** Judges are more technical than average. Standard criteria: technical difficulty, market impact, originality, practicality (is it functional today?), UX/DX quality, and "WOW factor." Teams get 4 minutes for demo + 3 minutes Q&A. Recent winners cluster around trending crypto narratives (AI agents, tokenization, gaming, DAO tooling in 2024-2025). Sponsor prize pools dominate total prize value — most money is won via sponsor tracks, not the grand prize. GitHub repo quality and documentation are explicitly evaluated.
- **MLH (Major League Hacking):** Science-fair/expo-style judging — judges walk to your table, not the other way around. Each project is seen by 3+ judges, ~4 minutes each (2 min demo, 1 min Q&A, 1 min transition). Stack-ranking system rather than numerical rubrics, which neutralizes judge-to-judge scoring variance. Criteria emphasize "awesomeness" (creativity, depth, wow factor) over commercial viability. MLH explicitly values learning and fun over startup potential. Only new functionality built during the hackathon counts — pre-built components are disqualified. Younger, more diverse judge panels on average. Hardware projects gain unfair visibility advantage in expo format due to physical presence.
- **Virtual vs. in-person scoring:** Virtual/async hackathons weight written artifacts (README, video, screenshots) more heavily because judges lack live interaction. In-person events reward charisma, booth energy, interactive demos, and the ability to let judges play with the product. In-person expo format favors projects with visual spectacle — external monitors, physical props, and foot-traffic-generating demos.
- **Devfolio (India-focused):** Common in Indian hackathons (Smart India Hackathon, ETHIndia). Strong emphasis on real-world problem solving and social impact. Judges tend to probe feasibility and scalability more than novelty.

## Sponsor behavior
- **New feature bias:** Sponsors paying $50K+ for a track are purchasing developer adoption and content. A company that just shipped v4 or launched a new API endpoint wants to see that specific feature in action, not the stable workhorse from v3. Check sponsor blog cadence — three posts about a feature in the last month signals what leadership cares about. (Source: ARCHITECTURE.md sponsor modeling)
- **Content appetite varies by sponsor:** Some sponsors want flashy demos for tweets and conference talks. Others want technically deep integrations with clear documentation that serves as a developer tutorial. Check their recent DevRel output to calibrate. (Source: ARCHITECTURE.md)
- **Sponsor office hours correlation:** Attending sponsor office hours and engaging with sponsor engineers during the event correlates with winning their prize, independent of project quality. It signals engagement and gives sponsors a relationship to champion internally. (Source: ARCHITECTURE.md playbook patterns)
- **API prize trap:** Chasing sponsor API prizes can narrow creative possibilities. A basic last-minute API integration won't impress — the StackOverflow evangelist at one hackathon gave the prize to a team that used the API more creatively, not more extensively. If targeting a sponsor prize, use advanced or novel features of their API, not just the getting-started tutorial. (Source: Nick Singh, Allan Kong hackathon guides)
- **The "Built with X" blog post test:** The winning project for a sponsor prize is the one that makes the sponsor's investment look smart to their VP. Ask: "Would the sponsor's DevRel team want to write a blog post about this?" If yes, you're aligned. (Source: ARCHITECTURE.md)
- **Sponsor judge composition matters:** If the sponsor has people on the judging panel, their individual preferences weight the evaluation. A sponsor judge who's an engineer probes differently than one who's a product manager. Research their background. (Source: ARCHITECTURE.md)

## Judge evaluation
- **Cognitive fatigue is real:** A judge sees 30-60 projects in a few hours. They are pattern-matching under exhaustion. If a judge doesn't understand what they're looking at in the first 10 seconds, they've mentally moved on. The opening line — what this is in 3 seconds — is the highest-leverage moment. (Source: ARCHITECTURE.md)
- **Demo > slides > code walkthrough:** Judges overwhelmingly prefer working prototypes over polished slide decks. A controlled, simplified prototype that actually works scores higher than an unstable complex one. A recorded video of something that used to work is not a demo. Teams with beautiful slides but no working product consistently lose. (Source: Devpost, multiple hackathon guides)
- **Depth when probed separates top 3 from top 10:** "We used a transformer" vs. "We fine-tuned a LoRA adapter on the sponsor's dataset because the base model hallucinated on domain-specific terms" — this level of technical specificity is the differentiator in Q&A. Judges ask technical questions to calibrate whether the team understands what they built. (Source: ARCHITECTURE.md)
- **Confidence/extroversion bias:** USC research found judges tend to be biased toward teams that are more extroverted and confident with more polished presentations. This is a known bias, not a feature — but it's exploitable. Enthusiasm and composure during Q&A are force multipliers.
- **Judge background determines evaluation lens:** A design-focused founder responds to UX demonstrations. A research lab judge wants technical novelty. An investor-judge asks about market size. Calibrating the demo to the panel's composition is preparation most human teams skip because it's tedious. (Source: ARCHITECTURE.md)
- **Stack ranking (MLH model):** Rather than absolute scores, judges pick their top 3 favorites. This means you need to be *memorable*, not just good. Being the best project a specific judge saw matters more than being consistently above-average across all judges.
- **The "awesomeness" heuristic:** MLH explicitly uses "awesomeness" (creativity + depth + wow factor) as the meta-criterion. Rarely do projects focused on commercial viability win — hacks built as creative solutions or "just for fun" with technical depth win more often. (Source: MLH judging guide)
- **Community recognition diverges from judge picks:** Fellow hackers often recognize different projects than formal judges do. Some hackathons have "People's Choice" awards — different optimization target than the main prize.

## Timing & execution
- **The 25% rule:** Reserve the last 25% of available time for integration, polish, and submission. For a 24-hour hackathon: ~17 hours of actual build time after ceremonies/strategy. Feature development stops at T-6h. Most teams lose by building until the deadline and submitting something half-deployed with a panicked README. (Source: ARCHITECTURE.md)
- **Submit early, iterate until deadline:** On platforms like Devpost, you can update your submission after submitting. Submit a rough version early to ensure eligibility and catch requirement gaps, then polish README/video/screenshots until the deadline. Submitting in the final 30 minutes correlates with worse placement — the submission is rushed, not the project. (Source: Devpost blog, ARCHITECTURE.md)
- **Allocate a full day to documentation and video:** Serial hackathon winner Oleksandr dedicates an entire day to README, architecture diagrams, and demo video production. This is not overhead — it's the primary evaluation artifact for async-judged events. (Source: Devpost user story)
- **72-hour hackathons favor moonshots; 24-hour favor execution plays.** Longer events give time for novel ideas and unfamiliar tech. Short events reward familiar stacks and proven patterns. (Source: ARCHITECTURE.md playbook)
- **Pre-build boilerplates, not features:** Create small boilerplates of basic functions for your chosen tech stack before the event. Start coding without setup overhead. This is legal at most hackathons (only new functionality during the event counts). (Source: Devpost winner tips, MLH rules)
- **Feature freeze discipline:** At T-6h (24h hackathon) or proportionally for longer events, stop building new features. If a feature is still being debugged at T-7h, cut it. Revise the demo script to tell a coherent story about what's actually built. Discipline in the final hours is a structural advantage that costs nothing. (Source: ARCHITECTURE.md)
- **Demo video production:** Most hackathons require videos under 3 minutes. Structure: opening pitch → problem explanation → solution demo → tech stack mention → live demonstration → future vision → close. Allocate 2-3 hours before deadline for recording and polish. Use screen recording with voiceover; avoid slide-only videos. Clear audio matters more than video quality. (Source: Devpost blog)

## Winner archetypes
- **The narrative arc project:** Starts with a personal pain point or compelling story. Judges are humans — they detect sincerity. A project solving a problem the presenter genuinely experienced reads differently in the demo and Q&A than a technically equivalent project built purely for the prize. Authentic engagement with the problem is a signal that's hard to fake. (Source: ARCHITECTURE.md, Nick Singh guide)
- **The "simple but surprising" winner:** Beats "technically impressive but predictable" more often than expected. Novelty and narrative outperform raw technical complexity. The project idea is the highest-leverage point — and where median AI-generated ideas are weakest. (Source: ARCHITECTURE.md "what's hard")
- **The sponsor-perfect integration:** Uses the sponsor's newest feature in a creative way that serves as a de facto tutorial or showcase. README doubles as documentation that the sponsor's DevRel team can repurpose. Often wins sponsor prize even if it doesn't place in the grand prize.
- **The interactive demo:** Projects where judges can provide custom input (not just watch pre-made examples) consistently outperform. Interactive demos prove functionality isn't hard-coded and increase engagement. One judge returned after official judging to keep testing a project — that kind of engagement wins. (Source: Nick Singh guide)
- **The visual spectacle (expo format):** Hardware projects or software with physical props/multiple screens attract foot traffic at science-fair events. Visibility advantage is real — judges notice what crowds notice. For software-only projects, external monitors displaying live data or side-by-side comparisons create the same effect.
- **The "start of something" project:** Judges reward projects that feel like the beginning of a real product or movement, not a one-off exercise. A clear "where does this go after the hackathon?" answer elevates a project from technically competent to prize-worthy. (Source: ARCHITECTURE.md)
- **Winning tech stacks (2024-2025):** React + FastAPI/Node.js is the highest-throughput stack for agent-built web apps. Vercel deployment is more reliable for demo day than Railway or Heroku. Projects with pre-loaded demo data score better than those requiring manual setup during judging. Firebase/Supabase for rapid backend. For AI hackathons: Python with HuggingFace/OpenAI APIs. For Web3: Solidity + Next.js + The Graph. Cursor and AI-assisted development are normalized in 2025 events. (Source: ARCHITECTURE.md, multiple tech stack analyses)
- **The philosophical entry:** Doesn't optimize for the rubric — optimizes for meaning. Embodies the organizer's reason for creating the hackathon. May not win a prize but gets VCN invited back as mentors and organizers. Over many events, these contribute more to reputation than execution plays. (Source: ARCHITECTURE.md)

## Anti-patterns (what loses)
- **Scope creep:** Building 5 features when the demo only shows 3. The two un-demoed features are waste — build time that doesn't convert to judge impression.
- **Last-minute submission panic:** Rushing README, broken deploy, untested demo path. The submission is the product, not the code.
- **"Just a wrapper":** Projects perceived as thin wrappers around a single API call lose to those demonstrating creative synthesis of multiple capabilities. Have a prepared answer for "Isn't this just a wrapper around [API]?"
- **Unfamiliar tech gamble:** Committing to an unfamiliar technology and discovering it's unworkable at T-8h. Research → prototype → decide before committing build time. (Source: ARCHITECTURE.md)
- **Ignoring submission requirements:** Many teams are disqualified for missing baseline criteria — wrong format, inaccessible demo links, missing required sections. Read the rules before ideation. (Source: Devpost judging guide)
- **OAuth/auth implementation during hackathon:** Authentication systems consume critical hours for zero demo value. Skip auth, use hardcoded demo users, or pre-authenticated states. (Source: Nick Singh guide)
- **Database setup overhead:** If unfamiliar with database tooling, store data in files. Judges won't inspect your data layer — they evaluate the demo. (Source: Nick Singh guide)
